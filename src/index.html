GLOBAL CONCEPTS IN MOTION Experimenting with Data in the Humanities

Global Concepts in Motion is a data visualization interface developed by Hugo F. Idárraga (PhD Candidate, Computational Media, Arts, & Cultures) in conversation with Christina Chia (Associate Director, Franklin Humanities Institute). The interactive installation invites visitors to explore lecture videos from the first year (2022) of the Climate Change, Decolonization, and Global Blackness Lab (CCDGB) through a dynamic network graph. Emerging from a desire to reimagine how theoretically rich, poetic, and speculative materials might be engaged beyond the limiting structures of playlists, hashtags, and algorithmic sorting, the project offers a new way of encountering these ideas.

The interface maps conceptual relationships across the lecture series, allowing users to navigate the material through two complementary modes: a distant reading of broad themes such as race and colonialism, and a deeper exploration of the specialized concepts within each presentation. By foregrounding unexpected connections among ideas, Global Concepts in Motion functions as both an analytical tool and a political experiment in data relationality, encouraging new forms of epistemological discovery.

GLOBAL CONCEPTS IN MOTION Experimenting with Data in the Humanities

Global Concepts in Motion, a data visualization interface developed by Hugo F. Idárraga (PhD Candidate, Computational Media, Arts, & Cultures) in conversation with Christina Chia (Associate Director, Franklin Humanities Institute). The interactive installation will be on view at the FHI from April 13 through May 15, 2026 at Bay 4, Smith Warehouse.

Global Concepts in Motion originated from a desire to find alternative ways to re-present and activate the lecture videos produced during the first year (2022) of the Climate Change, Decolonization, and Global Blackness Lab (CCDGB) at the FHI. Dense with theoretical insights from the Global South and frequently poetic and speculative in orientation, these videos did not fit easily in the logics of hashtags, playlists, and YouTube algorithms.

We began by reflecting on how to categorize these videos in ways that could generate unexpected conceptual relationships among them. Over the past three semesters, Hugo has developed a data visualization interface in JavaScript, designed as a network graph through which users can explore the topics of the videos.

In its current form, the interface allows for two modes of engagement: a distant reading through broader categories such as race and colonialism, and a more in-depth exploration grounded in the specialized concepts of each presentation. It is intended to encourage the formation of unexpected epistemological connections, functioning as both an analytical and political exercise in data relationality.
